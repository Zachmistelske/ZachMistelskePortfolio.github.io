Observing Global Energy Consumption

As the demand for energy increases in the world with rising populations, it is important to observe trends in how much energy is being consumed by multiple sources. In this project, we will observe what types of energy sources are the most prominent in consumption per capita (kWh), which ones behave linearly such that predictions can be made, and if the use of fossil fuels is decreasing.

Introduction

By looking at multiple data sets for energy consumption by coal, oil, solar, wind, hydro, nuclear per capita (kWh) from the energy explorer on OurWorldInData.org, we will begin first by taking a look into which how much energy consumption is persisting overtime for each sources. Then find how much they contribute to the total world energy consumption for each source. Below is the cleaned excel file that shows the average and total energy consumption in kWh for each country, the percentages for each source, and how much each source is contributing to the respective countries.

Analysis

Based off of calculations, coal contibutes 23.7%, hydro at 35.1%, nuclear at 11.6%, oil at 29.0%, solar at 0.48%, and wind at 0.17% of energy consumed per capita in the world. This implies that fossil fuels such as coal and oil consists of just over half of the world's energy consumption per capita. The U.S. and Australia has the highest in coal percentage contributions in the world at 1.47% and 1.93% respectively. Conclusion

According to the forecasting, fossil fuel consumption such as coal and oil consumption is expected to decrease by the year 2030 where coal consumption is expected to reach the amount as the same as near 1989 and oil consumption as the same as near 1999. There is also an apparent linear trend in solar and wind energy consumption from the year 2011 to 2021. The energy source that experienced the largest change in percentage difference in world consumption was solar energy at 2,282.49% from 1988 to 1989. Energy sources such as coal, oil, and hydro all have experienced noticable decreases in percentage differences from 2020 to 2021 between 20%-90% further indicating that they are on the decline. Addressing the Global Climate Change Crisis

Emissions Linear Fit

Looking at the average MtCO2e emissions for all countries since 1991, the emissions seem to behave linearly in time. Thus, Linear-fit regression of the form y=a+bx was performed finding a good fitting model of y = 1945.2(9.4) + 0.297(0.049)x. Uncertainties marked with () and x is the average emissions with y being time in years. So, for emissions of 277.4 MtCO2e for the tableau graph in 2029, it was found that the year is 2027(23) which is up to percision of nearly 1.13% furhter implying the model agrees with the tableau forecasting.

Solar Energy Linear Fit

Looking back on the energy consumption project, it is apparent that the average solar energy per capita for all countries behaves linearly from 2011 to 2021. Thus, Linear fit regression of the form y = a + bx can be used for predictions of the year based on solar energy consumption per capita (kWh). The regression yeilded y = 2010.5(2.8) + 0.117(0.093)x. By plugging in the energy consumption per capita of 135.6 kWh in the year 2023 from the tableau forcast, yielded 2026(16) which is up to percision of nearly 0.8% furhter implying the model is in close agreement.
